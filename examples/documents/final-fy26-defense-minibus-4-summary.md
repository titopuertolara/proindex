## DEFENSE APPROPRIATIONS ACT, 2026

The Defense Appropriations Act provides a total discretionary allocation of $839.2 billion, and invests in America's military superiority, shapes a more efficient and effective Department of Defense, protects from threats at America's border, and takes care of our troops and their families.

## KEY TAKEAWAYS

## · Champions America's military superiority by:

- o Enhancing investments in 5th and 6th generation aircraft including the F-35, F-47, and F/A-XX.
- o Supporting the modernization of the nuclear triad: the B-21 Raider, the Columbia Class Submarine, and Sentinel.
- o Boosting production of munitions critical to winning conflicts with pacing adversaries like China.
- o Allocating approximately $13.4 billion for missile defense and space programs to augment and integrate in support of the Golden Dome effort.
- o Maximizing American production through funding the establishment of a Civil Reserve Manufacturing Network.
- o Accelerating hypersonic flight tests by providing $4.5 billion for hypersonic test infrastructure, testing, and weapons systems.
- o Scaling capacity of critical suppliers by allowing the Office of Strategic Capital to access over $4.3 billion in loans and loan guarantees.
- o Enabling increased competition through elevated Defense Innovation Unit and Accelerate the Procurement and Fielding of Innovative Technologies investments.
- o Providing $13.3 billion for Special Operations Forces operations, equipment, and capabilities development.

## · Cares for our troops and their families by:

- o Providing a 3.8% pay raise for all servicemembers.
- o Supporting the Administration's efforts to reduce Permanent Change of Station (PCS) moves to improve quality and stability of life for our troops.
- o Continuing historic enacted Fiscal Year 2025 pay increases for junior enlisted servicemembers.

## · Shapes a more efficient and effective Department of Defense by:

- o Cutting over $1.2 billion to reflect savings attributable to efficiencies, streamlining of functions, and management improvements in the Department of Defense.
- o Cutting $550 million to reflect savings due to favorable bulk fuel rates.
- o Advancing innovation efforts, increasing competition, and driving down costs.
- o Providing a 1% civilian employee pay increase, as directed under President Trump's Fiscal Year 2026 alternative pay plan.

<!-- image -->

- Combats international actors who facilitate drug manufacturing and trafficking into the country by:
- o Providing $1.1 billion for counter-drug programs.
- o Increasing funding for the National Guard Counter-Drug Program.
- o Including report language to maintain the current United States Southern Command headquarters to signal sustained United States commitment to the Western Hemisphere.
- Supports the Trump Administration and mandate of the American people by:
- o Ensuring no funding for diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) programs, Critical Race Theory, or abortion-related travel, consistent with Department of Defense policy.
- o Prohibiting the use of funds to destroy surplus firearms or spent brass that are no longer of use to the United States military.
- o Prohibiting funds to support the Wuhan Institute of Virology or EcoHealth Alliance.
- o Continuing legacy provisions to prevent the closure or transfer of detainees from Naval Station Guantanamo Bay, Cuba.

## DETAILED FUNDING SUMMARY

## Military Personnel

Provides $193.3 billion for active, reserve, and National Guard military personnel, which is $10.8 billion above the Fiscal Year 2025 enacted level.

- Provides for a total end strength of 1,302,800 active forces and 764,900 selected reserve forces.
- Delivers a 3.8% pay raise for all military personnel effective January 1, 2026 - in line with President Trump's agenda.
- Supports the Trump Administration's efforts to reduce PCS moves to improve quality and stability of life for servicemembers.

## Operation and Maintenance

Provides $294.4 billion for operation and maintenance support to the military services and other Department of Defense entities.

- Military Readiness
- o Supports day-to-day military readiness by robustly funding training, exercises, maritime activities, flying hours, equipment sustainment, and base operations across the Department of Defense.

<!-- image -->

- o $7.1 billion to sustain ship operations to continuously deploy combat-ready warships, including an additional $1.9 billion above the President's budget request.
- o $20.7 billion to Army, Navy, Marine Corps, Air Force, Space Force, and National Guard depot maintenance efforts to ensure servicemembers are equipped and prepared.
- o $18.8 billion for facility sustainment, restoration, and modernization to sustain targeted increases for installation maintenance, shipyard repairs, and other facility enhancements, including an additional $130 million to support Marine Corps Barracks 2030.
- o $55 million for U-2 programmed depot maintenance to fully restore three aircraft, and prevents the divestment of more than eight U-2 aircraft.
- o Delivers increases for the State Partnership Program to support individual state National Guard military-to-military engagements with partner nations around the world; bolsters Exercise Northern Strike, a National Guard Bureau exercise designed to build readiness; and supports Army National Guard and Air National Guard operational and personnel investments.
- o $79 million for the Civil Air Patrol operations and procurement to back emergency service missions, along with education and cadet programs, in support of the United States Air Force.

## · Support Programs

- o $60 million for Apex Accelerators to provide technical assistance to businesses interested in working with the Department of Defense.
- o $53 million for Starbase to support students participating in challenging science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM) exercises in support of the Department of Defense.
- o $197.6 million for National Guard Youth Challenge to get teenagers on track toward graduating high school - an investment increase of $87.4 million.
- o $4.3 million for United States Naval Sea Cadet Corps to develop American youth in seagoing skills.
- o $50 million for Impact Aid and $20 million for Impact Aid for children with disabilities to assist local school districts that have lost property tax revenue due to the presence of tax-exempt federal property, such as military installations.
- o $25 million for the Red Cross and $24 million for the United Service Organizations to support servicemembers and their families.
- o $5 million to support construction of additional Fisher Houses to meet the needs of military family members when confronted with illnesses or hospitalization.
- Military Cooperation
- o $342.5 million for the Counter-ISIS Train and Equip Fund.

- o $100.8 million for Overseas Humanitarian, Disaster, and Civic Aid for foreign disaster relief, humanitarian assistance, and the humanitarian mine action program.
- o Includes report language directing the Secretary of Defense to maintain the current United States Southern Command headquarters to signal sustained United States commitment to the Western Hemisphere.

Provides $167.5 billion for procurement to the military services and other Department of Defense entities.

## · Aircraft

- o $7.6 billion for 47 F-35 fighters, and an additional $440 million for F-35 and F135 spare parts.
- o $1.9 billion for B-21 Raider.
- o $1.9 billion for up to 14 CH-53K heavy transport helicopters.
- o $1.1 billion for three E-2D Advanced Hawkeye aircraft.
- o $976 million for six C-130Js for the Air National Guard.
- o $500 million for four KC-130Js for the Navy Reserve.
- o $387.7 million for three MQ-25 Stingray unmanned carrier-based aircraft.
- o $913 million for UH/HH-60M Army Blackhawk helicopters, and an additional $65 million for advanced procurement.
- o $629 million for CH-47F Block II Chinook helicopters, and an additional $61 million for advanced procurement.
- o $474 million for two Compass Call aircraft.
- o $240 million for MQ-1C Gray Eagle 25M aircraft for the Army National Guard.
- o $362 million for AH-64 Apache Block IIIA remanufacture.

## · Shipbuilding

- o $27.2 billion for 17 ships, including seven battle force ships, to include one Columbia-class ballistic missile submarine, two Virginia-class fast attack submarines, three Medium Landing Ships, and one TAGOS SURTASS ship for anti-submarine warfare.
- o $1.5 billion for the Maritime Industrial Base to invest in critical areas including supplier capacity and capability, strategic outsourcing, workforce training, and technology and infrastructure.
- o $800 million for three Medium Landing Ships to expedite fielding and delivery of capability to the Marine Corps.
- o $242 million for long lead items for FF(X) Frigate program in support of the Navy's objective of quickly delivering a small surface combatant to the Fleet.
- o $320 million for two Ship-to-Shore Connectors.

- Vehicles and Force Protection
- o $780 million for 105 Amphibious Combat Vehicles for the Marine Corps.
- o $715 million for M109A7 Paladin Self-Propelled Howitzers and M992A3 Field Artillery Ammunition Support vehicles.
- o $779 million to upgrade Abrams tanks to the M1A2 (SEP)v3 tank variant.

Provides $145.9 billion for Research, Development, Test and Evaluation to the military services and other Department of Defense entities.

- Aircraft
- o $3.9 billion for 6th Generation aircraft, including $972 million for Navy's Next Generation Fighter FA-XX, and $3 billion for Air Force's F-47.
- o $2.7 billion for Air Force's Long Range Strike Bomber.
- o $1.1 billion for Air Force E-7 Wedgetail.
- o $1 billion for Navy's Take Charge and Move Out mission.
- o $1.2 billion for Army's Future Long Range Assault Aircraft (MV-75).
- o $175 million for the Improved Turbine Engine Program.
- Space Systems
- o $3.8 billion for Missile Warning / Missile Tracking systems.

- o $2.5 billion in classified space programs to protect and defend United States space assets to ensure the United States has space superiority.
- o $1.8 billion for jam-resistant and wideband military satellite communications, including assured satellite communications for nuclear command and control.
- Innovation and Industrial Base
- o $177.4 million for the establishment of a Civil Reserve Manufacturing Network.
- o $4.5 billion for hypersonic test infrastructure, testing, and weapons systems.
- o $4.3 billion to allow access for Office of Strategic Capital loans and loan guarantees to scale capacity of critical suppliers.
- o $429.5 million for the Defense Innovation Unit (DIU).
- o $400 million for Accelerate the Procurement and Fielding of Innovative Technologies (APFIT), including $75 million for software-only solutions.
- o $812.1 million for Industrial Base Analysis and Sustainment support.

- o $321.9 million for Defense Production Act Purchases.

## Revolving and Management Funds

Provides $2.1 billion for the Defense Working Capital Funds account to execute maintenance and supply functions.

- Supports the National Defense Stockpile Transaction Fund to provide strategic and critical materials in times of national emergencies.
